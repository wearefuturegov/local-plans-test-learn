Deciding what evidence is relevant to your plan

There are some studies that all local plans must include, and others that are dependent on context or local planning authority (LPA) discretion. When you start to work on your plan, use this guidance to consider what studies you will need to produce.

To adhere to the National Planning Policy Framework and to ensure LPAs can produce a local plan within 30 months, the evidence supporting the plan needs to be:

  • relevant – you do not need to produce a study if it is not necessary for your local plan
  • proportionate – you should produce all the evidence needed for examination to prevent delays
  • up-to-date – you do not need to recreate evidence if it already exists and remains relevant

For more information on these principles, see the guidance on ensuring a plan is relevant, proportionate and sufficiently up-to-date.

Studies all LPAs should provide

Housing land availability assessment

This study is to identify:

  • identify how much land is potentially available for housing development in the area
  • select sites for development

It is also known as a strategic housing land availability assessment (SHLAA).

Do this study early on, as it will inform other parts of the evidence base such as the housing needs assessment.

This is often prepared alongside the economic land availability assessment as a Housing and economic land availability assessment.

The housing needs assessment should be prepared as part of the Spatial Development Strategy (SDS), unless there is not an SDS in place.

The housing and land availability assessment should include, or be done alongside:

  • Windfall study – if you are likely to rely on windfall sites as part of your supply of land for housing
  • Site selection methodology and site allocations
  • Availability of sites for large scale development
  • Housing trajectory - evidence to identify timelines for development for all sites coming forward

Housing need assessment

This study is to assess levels of need for different types of housing in the area. It is also known as a strategic housing market assessment (SHMA).

This is often prepared alongside the economic needs assessment as a Housing and economic needs assessment.

The housing need assessment should be prepared as part of the Spatial Development Strategy (SDS), unless there is not an SDS in place.

The housing needs assessment must include:

  • Housing needs of different groups
  • Housing for older and disabled people

Housing requirement study – likely alongside SHLAA

This study is to assess how many homes the plan should seek to provide

Economic needs assessment

This study is to assess levels of need for economic development in the area.

This is often prepared alongside the housing needs assessment as a Housing and economic needs assessment.

The housing need assessment should be prepared as part of the Spatial Development Strategy (SDS), unless there is not an SDS in place.

Economic land availability assessment (ELAA)

This study is to identify how much land is potentially available for economic development in the area.

Do this study early on, as it will inform other parts of the evidence base such as the housing needs assessment.

This is often prepared alongside the housing land availability assessment as a Housing and economic land availability assessment.

The housing and land availability assessment should include, or be done alongside:

  • Employment Land Review (ELR) - a survey and assessment of the quantity and quality of existing employment land.

Town Centre Strategy

This study is to identify:

  • hierarchy and geographical boundary of town centres
  • requirements for centres, including need for new allocations of land for town centre development

For more information, see the guidance on town centres and retail.

Heritage assessment

This study is to identify the main heritage features within the plan area.

For more information, see the guidance on historic environment.

The housing and land availability assessment should include, or be done alongside:

  • Archaeology assessment – an audit of architectural assets and features in the area

Strategic flood risk assessment – levels 1 and 2

This study is to to assess the risk to an area from flooding from all sources, now and in the future.

For more information, see the guidance on flood risk.

Additional studies your LPA may need to provide

Depending on your LPA’s context, you may also need to provide these studies.

Urban design and capacity study

Large urban authorities where identifying brownfield land capacity is important should provide this study. This may be done as part of the housing land availability assessment.

Leisure and tourism study

Provide this study if leisure and tourism likely to be important to the economy of your area.

Rural economy study

Provide this study if the rural economy is likely to be important to your area.

Density standards

If you are proposing to set density standards, provide evidence to justify their use and determine the figures for minimum density.

Coastal change management area study

Provide this study if your LPA has any areas of coastline that are likely to be affected by physical change. This study is to set out what types of development will be appropriate in these areas.

For more information, see the guidance on coastal change.
